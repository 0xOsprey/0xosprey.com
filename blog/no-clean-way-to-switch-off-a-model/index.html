No Clean Way to Switch Off a Model

Joe Coll  ·  Jun 16, 2026

A few days ago I wrote about a t-shirt. The point was that the government had pulled an old playbook off the shelf and aimed it at AI. The letter behind it was secret then. It is public now.

It does one thing. It lets a single letter switch off a frontier model worldwide, for everyone who is not American, with criminal penalties for ignoring it.

Everyone is reaching for the First Amendment. It is the exciting argument and the wrong one. Nobody reads model weights. The government already controls other piles of numbers nobody reads, and the courts have let it.

The argument that works is boring. The model was published. Published things are already outside these rules. You do not need the Constitution. You need the exception the rulebook wrote for itself.

Here is the real shape of it. There was no clean way to do this. Aim at the weights and you hit the exception. Aim at the model and you hit it harder, and pick a constitutional fight on top. Aim at the API and you invent a transaction that never happened. Every version is contestable. The letter is not the mistake. The instrument is.

So it will not go to court. Anthropic does not want to be the company that sued the security state, and it does not need to be. It gets negotiated away. A quieter Fable comes back.

Which means the legal fight was never the story. This is.

A model millions used on Tuesday can be switched off worldwide by Friday, by one letter, citing reasons the public cannot read. Access is now a permission. Granted quietly. Revocable in an afternoon.

The honest fix is a law, not a clever brief. It is the rare case where the company, the government, and the public all want the same thing. Almost nobody will pass it in time.

The letter became public via a Bloomberg copy reported June 16, 2026. It is an "is informed" letter under the Export Control Reform Act (50 U.S.C. 4817) and EAR 744.22, naming the Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 models. Published-information carve-out: 15 CFR 734.7 and 734.3(b). The carve-out reading follows Charlie Bullock's analysis of the letter. Deemed export doctrine: 15 CFR 734.13. Sorrell v. IMS Health Inc., 564 U.S. 552 (2011), on the creation and dissemination of information as protected speech.
