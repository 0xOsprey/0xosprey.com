Export Controls / Satire

The T-Shirt They Made Illegal

Joe Coll  ·  Jun 12, 2026

1995

The US government once put the RSA encryption algorithm on the same export-control list as cruise missiles. Strong cryptography — keys over 40 bits — was a "munition" under the International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR), administered by the State Department, with felony penalties for unauthorized export. The deemed export rule extended this to showing the technology to a foreign national on US soil. Their eyes on your screen: federal crime.

Cryptographer Adam Back designed a response. He printed a working RSA implementation on a t-shirt. The complete text of this "international munition" was approximately 220 characters.

At DEF CON — a conference packed with foreign nationals — wearing it was technically committing multiple counts of illegal arms export. That was the point. The code also appeared on coffee mugs, tattoos, and email signatures. Every distribution: another munitions shipment.

1999

Daniel Bernstein sued after the State Department told him he'd need to register as a munitions dealer to publish his dissertation. The Ninth Circuit ruled in 1999: source code is protected speech. You cannot apply prior restraint to mathematics written as software.

The Clinton administration moved cryptography from ITAR (weapons shelf) to EAR — the Export Administration Regulations, administered by the Commerce Department, the dual-use commercial technology framework. The cypherpunks had won.

What they had actually won: the government was forced to move math from the weapons shelf to the dual-use shelf. The controls didn't disappear. The bureaucratic container changed. The government filed a note: next time, start with EAR.

June 2026

Anthropic released Claude Fable 5 on June 9, 2026 — a Mythos-class frontier model sharing its weights with the restricted Claude Mythos 5, estimated at roughly 10 trillion parameters. The US government applied export controls to the Mythos 5 weights under EAR — the exact framework the cypherpunks forced them into in 1999. Anthropic reportedly restricted access globally to ensure compliance with the deemed export rule.

Same play. Different rulebook. The government remembered.

At 5,000 characters per shirt: 30 billion shirts — roughly four for every person alive on Earth. Laid flat, they'd cover an area larger than Antarctica.

If you made one giant shirt and used microscopic print (100 chars/cm², requires magnification): ~150 km² — approximately the size of Washington, D.C. At a legible 6pt font, the required fabric grows to ~750,000 km² — larger than Argentina. The DC figure is the absolute best case.

Why it's different now

The 1995 protest worked because the cypherpunks had a clean First Amendment argument: source code is human-readable expressive speech, and the government can't apply prior restraint to it. A court agreed.

Model weights are harder to defend. 10 trillion floating-point numbers have no expressive content a court will recognize in the conventional sense. The nuclear blueprint analogy applies: the government has always restricted nuclear weapon designs, which are also "just information," and no court has found that unconstitutional. If the weights are sufficiently capability-dense, the First Amendment argument may not reach them.

EAR is also harder to attack than ITAR. The dual-use framework — technologies with legitimate civilian applications that could also enable harm — is exactly the right conceptual frame for frontier AI. The government isn't calling this a weapon. They're calling it dual-use commercial technology, which is both legally defensible and, arguably, accurate.

The punchline

The 1995 protest worked because the weapon was public. RSA was mathematics published in academic papers. Anyone could print it. The absurdity was wearable.

Fable 5's weights are closed source. The "munition" lives in a data center, accessible only through an API, under export compliance requirements. You can't print what you can't see.

The cypherpunks won because math is small and free. Today's AI weights are neither. The shirt is the size of Washington, D.C. Nobody gets to wear it.
